RijksMuseum's history

HOW IT STARTED On November 19, 1798, the government decided to establish a national museum, following the French example. A 'prestige project' to foster love for the country but also to preserve important objects. On May 31, 1800, this National Art Gallery opened its doors for the first time at Huis Ten Bosch in The Hague. With over 200 paintings and historical objects, partly from the stadholder collections and partly from (disbanded) national institutions, such as the VOC. The first acquisition, The Swan by Jan Asselijn, cost 100 guilders and is still one of the Rijksmuseum's top pieces.

THE 'ROYAL MUSEUM â€˜ In 1808 Louis Napoleon was at the helm of the Kingdom of Holland. He moved the collections to Amsterdam, the new capital. And not to just any building but to the Paleis on the Dam, Amsterdam's former city hall. There they were brought together with the city's most important paintings, including Rembrandt's Night Watch. In 1809, the Royal Museum opened on the top floor of the palace.

THE 'RIJKS MUSEUM' With Willem I as the new king from 1813, the museum moved as the 'Rijks Museum' together with the national print collection from The Hague to the Trippenhuis, a 17th-century city palace on Kloveniersburgwal. To the displeasure of the director, all kinds of other collections were subsequently moved elsewhere. Objects from classical antiquity went to a new Museum of Antiquities in Leiden and objects and arts and crafts were assigned to the Cabinet van rarities In Haarlem, a separate museum for modern, 19th-century art was established in Paviljoen Welgelegen in 1838. Unlike in the Napoleonic era, the Rijks Museum received few major works.

A NEW BUILDING Fine museums were being built all over Europe. The Netherlands, too, deserved a truly national, proud museum building. The Trippenhuis was not suitable, so something new could be built. The architect, Pierre Cuypers, had designed a historic style for the Rijksmuseum, a mixture of Gothic and Renaissance and full of patriotic symbolism. After years of fuss - many people thought it was too medieval, too Catholic and not really Dutch enough - construction began in 1876. In 1885 it was officially opened. In addition to the existing collection, the Rijksmuseum housed almost all of the City of Amsterdam's older paintings, such as Rembrandt's Jewish bride, which the banker A. van der Hoop had bequeathed to the city. Art from Haarlem was also added, part of the Kabinet van Zeldzaamheden was given a permanent home and the print gallery was given its own space. A worthy collection in a magnificent building.

RENOVATION UPON RENOVATION Over the years, the Rijksmuseum building has undergone many renovations. Between 1904 and 1916, for example, rooms were added to the southwest side, the current Philips Wing. In the 1950s and 1960s, the original two inner courtyards were filled in to create more auditorium space. After 1945, Dutch History and Sculpture & Applied Art were housed in separate parts of the building. Attempts to create a separate museum for Dutch history and thus make the Rijksmuseum a true art museum failed. In the 1950s a department of Asian Art was created as a result of the incorporation of the collection of the Association of Friends of Asian Art. The 1970s were booming, with nearly one and a half million visitors a year. But the building itself increasingly failed to meet modern museum requirements. What next?

THE CIRCLE IS COMPLETE In the latest renovation (2003-2013), the building regained Cuypers' original structure. The building has been modernized but at the same time the interior has become more Cuypers' building again, in all its grandeur. Painting, crafts and history are no longer in separate parts of the building. Everything together forms one chronological story: the story of Dutch art and history, from the Middle Ages to the 20th century. Immediately in 2013, the museum attracted a previously unprecedented number of visitors, over two million, and it has remained so until the corona period, partly thanks to the many tourists. Because more than ever, we strive to be the museum of and for everyone.
